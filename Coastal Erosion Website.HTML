Coastal Erosion

Introduction to Coastal Erosion




Destructive waves cause coastal erosion. In destructive waves, the backwash (the wave’s movement down the shoreline) is stronger than the swash (the movement up a shoreline). This pulls material away from a cliff. Destructive waves cause notches to form. This undercuts the ground above, causing it to become unstable and collapse.



When waves approach the beach at an angle, their swash will carry sediments up the shore and their backwash will carry sediments back down. This transports sediments along the beach and is called longshore drift.



Destructive waves can cause erosion via 5 main processes:



Hydraulic Action: The force of a wave dislodges material from the cliff

Compression: Air enters cracks in the rock, which is trapped by higher tides. Waves crashing into the rock causes the air to compress and decompress. This causes the crack to propagate and bits of rock to fall.

Abrasion: Materials carried by waves are thrown against the cliffs, causing material to dislodge from the cliff.

Solution: When sea water dissolve rocks (such as chalk and limestone).

Attrition: Rocks in the sea collide, breaking apart and becoming more rounded





Hard Engineering Strategies



Rock Groyne
Rock groynes are long structures made of materials including concrete, wood, large boulders or metal built perpendicular to the shoreline and extending out to sea. They trap sediments deposited by longshore drift, widening the beaches so that they better dissipate wave energy.





Sea Walls
Concrete walls that are placed at the bottom of a cliff. They are curved to reflect wave energy back into sea.


Rock Armour


Rock armour are large boulders placed at the foot of a cliff that absorb wave energy.



Tetrapods

Tetrapods are 2-10 ton concrete tetrahedral blocks that dissipate wave energy by allowing water to flow around them.







Soft Engineering Strategies


Beach Nourishment
Beach nourishment is placing sediment (usually dredged offshore) on a beach. This creates a wider beach which better absorbs wave energy.


Sand dune stabilisation
Dunes are mounds of sand formed by wind. They are an important coastal management tool because they provide protection against waves. Planting vegetation means roots grow into the dunes and bind the sediment together. This makes it harder for the dunes to erode. Dune stabilisation can also be achieved by fencing the dunes.


Managed retreat
Allowing flooding or eroding but relocating vulnerable infrastructure.



Cliff Regarding

Cliff regarding can be achieved through different ways:

Changing the angle of the cliff or reducing cliff heights, improving its stability.

Bolting unstable rocks into the cliff to increase stability.

Eliminating surface run-off (the flow of water on land) and infiltration (water on the ground entering the soil).







Landforms





Cracks in the rock form via hydraulic action and abrasion.

As waves continue to attack this crack, it widens and forms a cave.

This cave expands and waves break through the headland, forming an arch.

The arch’s base widens and the material above becomes unstable and fails, forming a stack.

Waves causes notches to form at the base of the stack. This causes the stack to become unstable and fall into sea, leaving a stump (usually only visible in low tide).
